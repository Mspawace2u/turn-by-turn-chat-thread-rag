### 🔥 Core Treatment Priorities for Adult Nephrotic Syndrome / CKD

**1) Control Edema (Water Weight Gain)**

*   **Diuretics (water pills)** — usually:
    
    *   **Furosemide** (loop diuretic)
        
    *   Sometimes **spironolactone** or **metolazone**  
        They help kidneys shed excess fluid but need careful monitoring (BP, electrolytes).   [NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK470444/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)  
        

**2) Reduce Proteinuria (Protein Loss)**

*   **ACE inhibitors or ARBs**  
    These drugs reduce pressure in the kidney filter and _directly decrease protein spilling into urine_, which protects kidney function longer.   [NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK470444/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)  
    

**3) Blood Pressure + Cardiovascular Risk Control**

*   **Blood pressure targets** are tighter in CKD (~<130/80 or even <120 in some cases).   [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7015670/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)  
    
*   **Cholesterol management**:  
    Statins are recommended for most CKD patients 50+ to reduce heart disease risk and mortality.   [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7015670/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)  
    

**4) Address Mineral & Metabolic Issues**

*   Monitor electrolytes
    
*   Vitamin D / bone health
    
*   Check for risk of clots — nephrotic syndrome increases clotting risk, sometimes requiring anticoagulants.   [NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK470444/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)  
    

**5) Identify the _Underlying Cause_**

Nephrotic syndrome in adults is often due to specific **kidney diseases** like:

*   **Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)**
    
*   Minimal change disease
    
*   Membranous nephropathy
    
*   Diabetic kidney disease
    

A **kidney biopsy** (tissue sample) is often needed to find out which one it is — because treatment differs.   [NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK470444/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)  

**6) Immunosuppression When Appropriate**

If the syndrome is due to immune-mediated disease (not all are):

*   **Prednisone / corticosteroids** — still used in some adult NS cases.
    
*   **Calcineurin inhibitors** (like tacrolimus, cyclosporine)
    
*   **Mycophenolate**
    
*   **Rituximab / other biologics** — for steroid-resistant or frequent relapses.   [NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK470444/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)  
    

⚠️ These are _serious drugs_. They suppress the immune system and have risks — infection, blood pressure issues, metabolic side effects.

### 🧪 Minimal Change Disease (MCD)

Common in kids, less common but still seen in adults.  
➡ Usually steroids first.  
➡ If relapse: tacrolimus, mycophenolate, or rituximab.

Prognosis: often very responsive.

—

### 🧪 FSGS (Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis)

More common in adults.  
➡ ACE/ARB always.  
➡ Steroids sometimes.  
➡ Tacrolimus common alternative.  
➡ SGLT2 inhibitors increasingly used.

Prognosis: variable. Depends on response.

—

### 🧪 Membranous Nephropathy

Autoimmune-driven in many cases.  
➡ ACE/ARB + SGLT2 first.  
➡ If high-risk: Rituximab often first choice now.  
➡ Sometimes cyclophosphamide or tacrolimus.

This is where Rituximab shines.

### 🧪 Diabetic Kidney Disease

➡ ACE/ARB  
➡ SGLT2 inhibitors  
➡ GLP-1 meds sometimes  
No immune suppression needed.

➡ Treatment targets the underlying blood disorder.

### 🧪 Lupus-related

➡ Steroids + immune drugs like mycophenolate or cyclophosphamide.
