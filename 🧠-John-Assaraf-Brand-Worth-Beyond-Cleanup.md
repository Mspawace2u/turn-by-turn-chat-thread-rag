## Q: 
I’m feeling a bit overwhelmed and accepting responsibility for the results of a broken infrastructure I didn’t create. Do you have any good advice to help me accept the truth that my role is not to go around cleaning up piles of poop as if that’s what determines my worth as a person

## A: 
Here’s the bottom line: You are not defined by how well you clean up other people's messes—especially ones you didn’t create.

It’s natural to feel responsible, especially when your role is operations-driven and your instincts are oriented around fixing and improving. But accepting responsibility for a broken infrastructure someone else created doesn’t measure your value, competence, or worth.

Here's how you can reframe it practically:

1.  **Separate your worth from the mess**  
    Your worth isn’t tied to cleaning up other people’s chaos. You're an architect—not a janitor. You’re there to build a better system, not endlessly patch broken ones.
    
2.  **Clarify your actual responsibility**  
    Define clearly what your role involves and, more importantly, what it doesn’t. Set boundaries and communicate that clearly to everyone involved. Your role is to design solutions, not chase around after problems.
    
3.  **Think strategic, not reactive**  
    Focus on forward-looking solutions instead of just damage control. When you shift into proactive planning and structural improvements, you’ll feel more control and less overwhelm.
    
4.  **Release attachment to outcomes you can’t control**  
    Sometimes infrastructures fail because of decisions made long before you stepped into the role. Acknowledge that you can’t control past decisions, only influence future ones.
    
5.  **Recognize your strength in navigating chaos**  
    You have the skills and experience to improve things systematically. Your real value lies in your ability to see patterns, design efficient processes, and build something stronger—not in endlessly absorbing blame or covering others’ mistakes.
    

You're not the chaos—you’re the one who creates clarity from it. That's your zone of genius.

## Q: 
Yes, and…Sometimes that easier said than done as a late life unmasking AuDHD woman who has always been the one people ask to untangle to damn messes

## A: 
Exactly. It's a tough pattern to break, especially when you've spent decades being the "fixer" who untangles everyone's chaos. And as a late-diagnosed AuDHD woman, the unmasking journey isn't just about awareness—it's about redefining your identity away from being the "problem-solver-by-default."

The deeper truth here is this: You deserve relationships and professional interactions that value your brilliance without exploiting it. It’s hard—but necessary—to shift from rescuing people from their messes to empowering them to handle their own responsibilities.

Here’s how you might practically navigate this:

*   **Recognize your patterns compassionately**  
    Accepting that you tend to jump in because your brain craves stimulation or validation is okay—but remember, just because you _can_ doesn’t mean you _must_.
    
*   **Interrupt the automatic "Yes"**  
    When someone comes to you for rescue, slow down your response. Instead of reflexively agreeing, buy yourself time to consciously decide if this aligns with your real purpose, capacity, and mental health.
    
*   **Redirect your problem-solving genius strategically**  
    Use your ability to spot patterns and create infrastructure proactively, rather than reactively. Focus on designing clear processes and standards so others can clean up their own messes—or better yet, prevent them.
    
*   **Set boundaries clearly and explicitly**  
    Boundaries aren't about punishing others—they’re about honoring yourself. Clearly communicate what's within your scope and what's not. It’s uncomfortable, but it gets easier the more you do it.
    
*   **Lean into your AuDHD authenticity**  
    Unmasking is about giving yourself permission to exist exactly as you are, without constantly performing to prove your worth. You don’t have to earn respect by cleaning up after everyone—you already deserve it simply by showing up exactly as you are.
    

This shift is challenging, uncomfortable, and not linear. But it’s the pathway to finally building a life—and work—that honors you, your energy, and your true worth.
